# Recent Campus Master Plan Benchmarks

**Research date:** July 22, 2026  
**Scope:** Recent U.S. university campus plans, plus faith-based university, college, and seminary models. “Faith-based” is treated as an institutional and spatial model—not simply the presence of a chapel.

## Executive benchmark

The most transferable contemporary model is a **10–15-year living physical framework**, refreshed about every five years and tied to a separate funded capital plan. It prioritizes stewardship and reuse before expansion, organizes work by districts and catalyst projects, and tracks a small number of annual indicators.

Faith-based campuses add five distinguishing design commitments:

1. **Sacred center and contemplative network** — worship is visible, but prayer, reflection, and landscape moments are distributed throughout daily campus life.
2. **Residential formation** — housing is a community-building and mentoring system, not only a bed count.
3. **Hospitality and belonging** — commons, dining, outdoor rooms, and thresholds support formal and informal gathering.
4. **Stewardship as mission** — reuse, landscape care, water, energy, and maintenance are framed as ethical responsibilities.
5. **Service beyond the boundary** — campus edges, trails, community rooms, and partnerships make the institution a neighbor and civic resource.

### What the general university cohort establishes

- **10–15 years is the practical planning horizon.** Thirty-year diagrams are useful for land protection and infrastructure, but near-term capital priorities should sit inside a shorter window.
- **A five-year formal refresh is a strong governance benchmark.** Annual reporting should update indicators and projects without reopening the whole plan.
- **Stewardship now precedes growth.** Recent plans explicitly consider reuse, renovation, utilization, consolidation, and selective removal before adding gross square feet.
- **Capital authorization stays separate.** A master plan identifies the right moves and protects options; it should not imply that every illustrated project is approved or funded.
- **Engagement should be measurable.** For a medium-to-large institution, roughly **1,000–4,000 broad-plan responses**, supplemented by targeted focus groups and interviews, is a defensible recent benchmark.

## C. Religious campus models to test

These are complementary models; a campus can combine several.

| Model | Spatial structure | Best suited to | Precedents in the benchmark set |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Sacred core** | Chapel, worship space, or sacred landscape anchors the main axis or central quad; everyday routes encounter the institution’s mission | Institutions with a strong common worship identity | Notre Dame; traditional Catholic campus form |
| **Contemplative network** | Small prayer rooms, gardens, groves, memorials, and quiet rooms distributed across academic and residential districts | Larger or more religiously diverse campuses where one central space is insufficient | Notre Dame’s sacramental/contemplative network; Baylor’s landscape framework |
| **Residential formation village** | Residence halls or houses form small communities around commons, dining, mentoring, and worship | Residential colleges and institutions emphasizing whole-person formation | Notre Dame; TCU; Grace College |
| **Hospitality commons** | A highly legible indoor/outdoor commons links worship, learning, dining, and community use | Campuses that host congregations, conferences, visitors, or community programs | Grace College; Wesley; Baylor gathering spaces |
| **Service-and-city bridge** | Trails, riverfronts, community rooms, clinics, and active edges connect institutional mission to civic life | Urban campuses and institutions with an explicit service mission | Baylor–Waco/Brazos connections; TCU–Berry Street/Trinity River; USD neighborhood planning |
| **Right-sized seminary or ministry hub** | Reuse and consolidate buildings; colocate administration, teaching, worship, housing, and community functions; preserve buffers | Small institutions with changing enrollment, aging facilities, or valuable land | Wesley Theological Seminary |

## D. Recommended benchmark targets for a new plan

These are proposed targets synthesized from the cases, not universal standards.

| Planning dimension | Recommended target |
|---|---|
| Core physical-plan horizon | **10–15 years** |
| Long-range land/infrastructure protection | **20–30 years** |
| Formal plan refresh | Every **5 years** |
| Implementation reporting | **Annual**, using a public scorecard |
| Capital phases | Near term **0–5**, mid term **5–10**, long term **10+ years** |
| Broad engagement | Target **10–20% of campus population** where feasible; report counts and constituency mix, not only the number of meetings |
| Investment screen | Mission alignment; utilization; condition/deferred maintenance; accessibility; carbon/water; student success; community impact; cost and funding readiness |
| Space strategy | **Reuse/renovate first**, then consolidate or remove low-value space, then build new only for a demonstrated program or capacity gap |
| Mobility | Track mode split, pedestrian and bicycle safety, transit use, parking demand, and universal access; set a declining SOV target appropriate to context |
| Housing | Track beds, percent housed, affordability, residency goals, utilization, and the link between housing type and community/formation outcomes |
| Faith and mission | Require each capital project to document how it supports worship/reflection, formation, hospitality/belonging, stewardship, and service |

## E. Suggested annual scorecard

A master plan becomes credible when it can report outcomes. A concise annual dashboard should include:

- **Portfolio:** total GSF, GSF/student, space utilization, facility condition, deferred-maintenance backlog, renovation versus new-build spending, and space removed or repurposed.
- **Student life:** beds, occupancy, percent of students housed, housing cost, dining/commons utilization, and student belonging/well-being indicators.
- **Mobility and access:** mode split, parking utilization, pedestrian/bicycle incidents, accessible-route completion, and average walking time between high-demand destinations.
- **Stewardship:** operational energy and carbon, water, stormwater treated, tree-canopy condition, biodiversity, waste diversion, and maintenance funding.
- **Mission:** access to worship and contemplation, participation in community/service programs, number and use of hospitality/community spaces, and preservation of sacred or historic assets.
- **Delivery:** projects completed, budget and schedule performance, external funding/donor participation, and progress by near-, mid-, and long-term phase.

## Bottom line

The best benchmark is not the campus with the largest building program. It is the institution that can show a direct chain from **mission and strategy → space and landscape decisions → funded phases → annual outcomes**. For a religiously based campus, the physical plan should make faith legible through daily experience—community, reflection, hospitality, stewardship, and service—rather than relying on architectural symbolism alone.

## F. Additional benchmarks for an interfaith campus in a Southern Indiana county of approximately 80,000

This context changes the comparison set. The campus should be evaluated as a **countywide and potentially multi-county community institution**, not only as a collection of religious buildings. The numbers below are recommended planning tests synthesized for this context; they are not universal code requirements.

### 1. Catchment and demonstrated demand

County population alone is a weak predictor of use. Map **15-, 30-, and 45-minute drive-time catchments**, including adjoining counties and any nearby metropolitan edge. Measure:

- Population, households, age, income, disability, vehicle availability, and forecast change in each catchment.
- Congregations, adherents, and average attendance by tradition. The 2020 U.S. Religion Census is the most useful county-level baseline, but it should be supplemented by a local survey because it does not fully describe unaffiliated residents or informal communities. [U.S. Religion Census county data](https://usreligioncensus.org/index.php)
- Existing worship, meeting, education, food-service, counseling, recreation, and event capacity within the 30-minute catchment.
- A realistic annual unique-user scenario: test **2–5% of county population** for a modest shared facility, **5–10%** for a strong countywide hub, and **10%+** only if programming demonstrably draws from a wider region.
- At least **60–70% of projected routine users within a 30-minute trip**, unless the campus is intentionally regional.

### 2. Interfaith governance—not just interfaith architecture

Governance is a core campus-planning benchmark because it determines access, symbolism, scheduling, funding, and conflict resolution.

The strongest direct precedent is Omaha’s Tri-Faith Commons: a synagogue, church, mosque, and interfaith center on **38 acres**. Its 25-member board is composed of **five representatives from each of three faith partners plus ten community members**—40% of the board is therefore independent community representation. The reported capital scale is approximately **$65 million**, making it a governance and program precedent rather than a direct cost or size comparator for an 80,000-person county. [Tri-Faith overview and governance](https://www.trifaith.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/ed-deck_5-2.pdf)

Recommended governance tests:

- No single faith tradition controls a voting majority.
- Reserve **25–40% of governing seats** for community, civic, educational, health, or nonprofit representatives who are not delegates of a resident faith partner.
- Publish policies for scheduling, permanent and temporary symbols, food practices, alcohol, weddings and funerals, proselytizing, political activity, security, and dispute resolution before design is finalized.
- Track board diversity, partner retention, schedule disputes, resolution time, and user perceptions of welcome and fairness.

### 3. A “distinct plus shared” physical model

Avoid forcing every religious practice into one generic room. Benchmark a three-part system:

1. **Tradition-specific or reservable worship space** where orientation, sound, ritual objects, seating, and sacred character can be appropriate.
2. **Religiously neutral shared space** for dialogue, education, meals, service, arts, and civic events.
3. **Distributed quiet/reflection rooms and landscape spaces** open to people of all faiths and none.

Recommended space tests:

- Target **20–35% of enclosed program area as genuinely shared space**, excluding corridors and building services; otherwise the development risks becoming colocated institutions rather than an interfaith campus.
- Provide secure tradition-specific storage so shared rooms can return to a neutral condition.
- Resolve prayer orientation, washing/ablution, acoustic separation, footwear, dietary preparation, candle/incense use, and gender/privacy needs during programming.
- Size daily-use spaces to routine attendance and use movable partitions or adjacent commons for peak holy days; do not size the entire campus around a few annual peaks.
- Include an outdoor gathering space and quiet landscape sequence. These are often the least doctrinally constrained places for encounter.

The University of Exeter’s 2025 multifaith center illustrates a useful hybrid: flexible reflection and event space, a dedicated **50-person Muslim prayer room**, and a home for multifaith chaplains, rather than one undifferentiated worship room. [Exeter Multifaith Centre](https://www.exeter.ac.uk/about/vision/capitalstrategy/featuredprojects/multifaithcentre/)

### 4. Utilization across the full week

A rural or small-county campus cannot depend on one congregation’s weekly peak. Develop an hourly operating model for weekdays, evenings, Fridays, Saturdays, Sundays, and major holy days.

Suggested stabilized targets:

- **50–65% utilization of reservable shared rooms during defined prime hours.**
- **30–45% utilization across all available operating hours.**
- At least **40% of public program hours co-produced** with schools, nonprofits, health providers, cultural organizations, or community groups.
- No more than **50% of shared-room prime time** routinely controlled by one partner.
- Report unique users, repeat users, room-hours, cancellations, denied requests, attendance by program type, and participation across traditions and nonreligious residents.

Prayer and contemplation rooms should be measured by **reliable availability, peak conflicts, and user satisfaction**, not by classroom-style occupancy alone.

### 5. County-serving program mix

For an 80,000-person county, the campus becomes more viable when it fills documented service gaps. Benchmark the program against local capacity for:

- Food pantry, community meals, teaching kitchen, and disaster feeding.
- Counseling, grief support, caregiver support, recovery groups, and social-service navigation.
- Youth and senior programs, after-school learning, arts, lectures, and civic dialogue.
- Volunteer coordination and nonprofit incubator/shared offices.
- Outdoor recreation, gardens, trails, and community festivals.
- Emergency shelter, warming/cooling, charging, communications, and supply distribution.

Require memoranda of understanding with service partners before dedicating specialized space. Track annual volunteer hours, people served, referrals completed, community-program attendance, and value of donated/shared space.

### 6. Transportation and accessibility

In Southern Indiana, access is likely to be more automobile- and demand-response-dependent than at the university precedents. The design should therefore test transportation equity rather than assuming everyone drives.

- Map current county or regional transit, senior transportation, paratransit, and congregation vans. INDOT administers rural transit programs, and Southern Indiana includes both countywide and regional providers. [Indiana rural transit programs](https://www.in.gov/indot/multimodal/transit/rural-transit-programs/) and [Indiana public transit systems](https://www.in.gov/indot/multimodal/transit/indiana-public-transit-systems/)
- Track the percentage of county residents and projected users who can reach the site in 15, 30, and 45 minutes by available modes.
- Share parking among partners and size it using **overlapping event calendars**, not the sum of each institution’s independent peak.
- Provide a covered drop-off, accessible paths, senior-friendly walking distances, bicycle connections where realistic, and space for vans or small shuttles.
- Establish a transportation-assistance program and measure rides provided and events inaccessible without private vehicles.

### 7. Financial and operational durability

The critical rural benchmark is not capital fundraising alone; it is whether the county can support operations, maintenance, staffing, and renewal.

Test:

- A base operating plan that does not require optimistic event revenue.
- No single congregation, donor, or earned-revenue stream supplying more than **35–40% of recurring operations**, unless a binding long-term agreement protects the campus.
- **Three to six months of operating reserves** at opening, growing toward six months.
- A funded annual renewal reserve tied to building and site replacement value.
- A lifecycle model covering at least **20 years**, including roofs, paving, technology, security, kitchens, landscape, and specialized worship systems.
- Shared staffing ratios and clear responsibility for maintenance, event setup, security, custodial work, and pastoral/chaplaincy coordination.

Indiana’s Office of Community and Rural Affairs supports community facilities, public gathering places, historic structures, and rural planning; USDA Rural Development also has an Indiana Community Facilities program for eligible essential community facilities. Eligibility will depend on ownership, use, and public benefit, so these should be investigated early rather than assumed. [Indiana OCRA](https://www.in.gov/ocra/about/) and [USDA Community Facilities—Indiana](https://www.rd.usda.gov/programs-services/community-facilities/community-facilities-direct-loan-grant-program-7)

### 8. Resilience as a community role

Southern Indiana planning should explicitly address tornadoes, severe storms, flooding, extreme heat, winter outages, and—depending on the precise site—seismic risk. Indiana reports more than **1,500 verified tornadoes since 1950**. IDHS promotes community safe rooms designed to FEMA criteria and notes that such rooms can be designed for winds up to **250 mph**. [Indiana tornado preparedness](https://www.in.gov/dhs/get-prepared/nature-safety/tornadoes/) and [IDHS community safe rooms](https://www.in.gov/dhs/emergency-management-and-preparedness/mitigation-and-recovery/community-safe-rooms/)

Recommended tests:

- Evaluate a FEMA-compliant safe room sized for the maximum routine or assembly occupancy.
- Keep critical buildings and access routes outside mapped flood hazards where possible.
- Provide backup power for life safety, communications, refrigeration, a warming/cooling area, and essential food or medical functions.
- Designate the campus’s emergency role in the county hazard-mitigation and emergency-operations framework.
- Conduct at least one multi-partner emergency exercise annually.

### 9. Success measures specific to the mission

Add these indicators to the annual master-plan scorecard:

- Number of participating faith and non-faith partners.
- Percentage of programs involving more than one tradition.
- Participation by first-time users and residents without a congregational affiliation.
- User-reported belonging, safety, fairness, and meaningful contact across differences.
- Shared-space utilization and equitable access to prime times.
- Volunteer hours, community services delivered, and households served.
- Operating subsidy per unique user and per occupied room-hour.
- Energy, water, maintenance, and renewal cost per square foot.
- Schedule conflicts, bias or safety incidents, and resolution outcomes.
- Economic contribution through local purchasing, employment, and visitor spending.

### Tailored conclusion

For this project, the most relevant benchmark is not “How many buildings can an 80,000-person county support?” It is: **Can the campus create more shared community value than the same partners could create separately, while preserving authentic religious practice and remaining affordable to operate?** The master plan should demonstrate that answer through catchment demand, balanced governance, shared-space ratios, a seven-day utilization model, service partnerships, lifecycle funding, and a defined resilience role.
